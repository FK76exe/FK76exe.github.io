Why did I create a website?

This isn't the first personal website I've launched. My first website was developed and deployed through GitHub Pages for two reasons (I think? This was in 2020 and I don't have a good memory):

  1. An exercise on front-end software development after a course I took that taught front-end technologies like HTML, CSS, JavaScript, jQuery, and Bootstrap
  2. To serve as a portfolio for employers

It contained only three pages:

  1. A landing page with a few buttons leading to other pages
  2. An about me page, containing a few sentences of who I am, what I do, what I'm doing
  3. A projects page, containing a series of blurbs of project links and a brief description of the project

It wasn't a lot, and frankly wasn't impressive (granted I was a first-year CS student, but still). I privatized that one a while back, preferring to start from scratch instead of refactoring it.

So why did I create this website?

There were several motivations for having this website up and running. I wanted a way to express myself publicly, and I didn't feel that platforms such as Twitter or Reddit were ideal for me.

While there are good content on both platforms and its contemporaries, there are a couple of things that turn me off:

1. Disinformation and Toxicity

I left Twitter in 2021 and Reddit in 2023. Since then, social media platforms seemed to become a lot more toxic in recent years (especially Twitter). Misinformation is rampant and purposefully engineered to manipulate and anger people, created by people for their benefit and promoted by platforms as negative content tends to gain more attention. A good and recent example is a fake video of a Donald Trump mail-in ballot being destroyed in Pennsylvania, as seen by this Reuters article. Thus, I do not think these platforms are a safe space for me to deliver my opinions on whatever I want.

2. Spam and Slop

Generative AI certainly has its perks (I've used it to create practice questions to prepare for upcoming midterms and exams in university), but I would not call its effect on social media positive. Because of how easy it is to generate and post AI content (not all AI content looks "bad", but the vast majority to me doesn't seem impressive and look goofy), it resulted in a lot of spam and uninformative content. It adds to point #1 as it makes misinformation and ragebait more plentiful and distributable. So why should my timeline be full of AI-generated content when the advertised purpose of social media was to connect and share with other people?

3. Being at the hands of "The Algorithm"

Likes, views, impressions, interactions, upvotes, whatever - these are metrics used by content platforms to piece together a list of content to recommend to a user. On its own, it doesn't seem so bad. After all, why shouldn't the most relevant content be recommended to you? My second point comes into play here: these metrics can be artificially inflated with zero human traffic (either by bots or buying views and subscribers), which renders these recommendation algorithms to be abused and promote misleading content. Consequently, to make yourself stand out to the algorithms you may be forced to sacrifice your original content. A good example lives on Instagram: If you used the platform recently, you'd surely have encountered posts where the description is an AI-generated overview of a car (the CLK GTR and Cybertruck are two prominent examples) or a baked good recipe like brownies and cake. Therefore, the influence of the algorithm and its rampant abuse renders it as not being this one-size-fits-all solution.

Leading to…

Based on my observations from experience and seeing whatever is shared with me, most traditional social media platforms are toxic, lack authenticity, and content is at the mercy of an all-powerful yet easily abused algorithm. To provide myself a space on the internet to discuss my profession and interests, a personal website under my control seemed like the best bet. I was particularly motivated by "you STILL need a website RIGHT NOW!! (yes, even in 2024)" by Network Chuck and "Basic Guide To The Personal Web", persuaded by the refuge it provides my content from power-hungry moderators and social media bots and trolls.

However, if there is a platform out there that has a positive community with a relatively smaller amount of drama and nonsense, I would be open to making my presence there known.
